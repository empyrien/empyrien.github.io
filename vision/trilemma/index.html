The Test

Liar, lunatic — or what He claimed to be?

C.S. Lewis argued Jesus's claims foreclose the comfortable middle. The trilemma is arguably cleaner for Bahá'u'lláh: His claim survives in His own handwriting, His life documented by hostile state records and Western observers. There is no “legend” escape hatch.

“Not of Mine own volition have I revealed Myself, but God, of His own choosing, hath manifested Me.”
Bahá'u'lláh, Epistle to the Son of the Wolf

Liar?

What did He gain?

A wealthy nobleman's son who refused a ministerial post, He spent forty years (1852–1892) as prisoner and exile — the Síyáh-Chál dungeon, Baghdad, Constantinople, Adrianople, 'Akká — and never recanted. “I have been, most of the days of My life, even as a slave, sitting under a sword hanging on a thread.”

He disclaimed leadership — “This Wronged One hath never had, nor hath He now any desire for leadership” — and forbade followers to spread miracle stories. Deceivers optimize for gain; His entire incentive gradient ran the other way.

Lunatic?

The shape of the output

“Well nigh a hundred volumes” by His own tally — roughly 15,000–20,000 extant tablets: mystical treatises, legal codes, letters to the kings of the earth, a blueprint for global institutions, produced across forty years mostly in prison, with sustained coherence. That profile is not what psychosis looks like.

Cambridge orientalist E.G. Browne, who met Him in 1890 and never became a believer: “Those piercing eyes seemed to read one's very soul; power and authority sat on that ample brow… No need to ask in whose presence I stood.”

Lord?

The remaining door

Then weigh what He claimed: to be the Promised One of every tradition, the Mouthpiece of God for the age of humanity's maturity — a claim made in writing, sustained for forty years under the sword, sealed without retraction, fortune, or throne.

Tolstoy, 1908: the teachings “present us with the highest and purest form of religious teaching.”

The skeptic's reply — and the answer

The standard objection to any trilemma: the options aren't exhaustive — a claimant could be honestly mistaken, sincerely convinced of a divine calling while being neither deceiver nor insane. The objection deserves an answer rather than silence, so here it is.

First, the “mistaken” hypothesis has to carry the same weight as the ones it replaces: a fixed false belief held for forty years under sword and dungeon, producing a hundred volumes of coherent law, philosophy, mysticism, and institutional design — addressed, without flinching, to the kings and emperors of the earth. At some point “sincerely mistaken” stops being the cheap exit it appears to be and starts requiring its own extraordinary evidence.

Second, consider the base rate honestly. The figures who made precisely this claim and sustained it to the end — Moses, Christ, Muhammad — did not found delusions; they founded the moral civilizations of history. Whatever this class of claimant is, “liar” and “lunatic” have been catastrophically poor predictors of what follows them. The recurring pattern is that such a Voice appears, is dismissed by exactly these arguments, and is vindicated by the civilization that grows from His words. To dismiss a new instance cheaply is not skepticism — it is ignoring the data history has already supplied.

So the trilemma, honestly framed, is an invitation to weigh a life — and ask which explanation carries it most naturally.

Sources: Epistle to the Son of the Wolf · Browne, A Traveller's Narrative (1891) · Stendardo, Leo Tolstoy and the Bahá'í Faith · Lewis, Mere Christianity
